Fascia & Plastics — Approach

Real-time operational visibility — across every OEE dimension

The goal is live, actionable visibility into everything that affects OEE in the Fascia & Plastics area: cycle time performance, uptime and downtime, and quality — drillable to a specific asset, vehicle model, option, or color. That requires pulling from multiple data pillars: Cimplicity, Inspect, FAULT, and the Help/Call system.

The approach

Real-time OEE — built on the data that already exists

The Fascia & Plastics engagement is focused on operations visibility: cycle time performance, uptime vs. downtime, and quality yield — visible in real time to everyone from team member to senior manager. The pilot area includes a full paint line, punch, and subassembly operations that feed directly into Trim and Final. The data already exists across Cimplicity, FAULT, and Inspect. The missing layer is a structured, unified way to surface it — and a Help/Call system that acts automatically when equipment stops.

Cimplicity + FAULT — cycle time & downtime

Cimplicity provides the equipment cycle signal — actual JPH vs. target, by asset and area. FAULT carries the downtime record and root-cause attribution. Together they give supervisors the availability picture in real time, not after the shift summary.

Inspect — quality yield

Connecting Inspect quality events to the specific asset, shift, and vehicle/option/color turns a defect count into a root-cause signal. First-pass yield becomes a live OEE input — not a lagging report.

Pilot

Fascia pilot — paint, punch & subassembly feeding Trim and Final

The pilot scope covers the full Fascia & Plastics area: paint line, punch machines, and subassembly operations — all of which feed directly into Trim and Final. Getting OEE visibility right here, with these assets and data sources, establishes the foundation and lessons learned that scale to every connected area — upstream and downstream.

Help/Call — automatic & visible

When Cimplicity detects a stop state, Help/Call should trigger automatically — no waiting for someone to notice and call. Open calls, response time, and resolution are visible across the area. This is the highest-priority adoption unlock: it makes the system useful to the people on the floor.

Scale out — built in from the start

The architecture, data connections, and OEE methodology defined in this pilot are designed to extend to other connected areas without rebuilding. Trim and Final, and areas beyond, inherit the same backbone — not a one-off solution that has to be re-discovered each time.

Pilot → scale: Fascia & Plastics is the starting point, not the end state. Paint line, punch, and subassembly feed Trim and Final — which feeds the rest of the plant. Getting the data foundation right here is how it scales. And every data connection feeds Nissan's longer-term direction: a functional digital twin of the facility.
Engagement structure & timeline

Six phases → one functional specification

The engagement is structured as a functional specification effort — not an implementation. Six phases over approximately eight weeks, each ending with a Nissan review checkpoint. The deliverable is a build-ready spec that makes follow-on implementation estimable, low-risk, and grounded in real current-state data from the Fascia floor.

Phase 1 — 1 day

Kickoff & alignment

Confirm scope, stakeholders, access, and the priority order for which area to tackle first. Shawn, Craig, and Paige aligned on how this connects to the control room work. Loop in data/systems engineering contacts.

Outcome: shared understanding of scope and sequencing before any discovery work begins.
Phase 2 — 2 weeks

Current-state mapping

Document existing systems, data owners, naming conventions, and where FAULT data actually lives vs. where it needs to go. Identify the entry points DRS uses, where the Japan format gap occurs, and the as-is connectivity picture for each equipment type.

Outcome: accurate as-is picture before requirements are written.
Phase 3 — 1 week

Feasibility & OEE requirements

Assess what injection molding machines, welder PCS, and punch machines can expose — protocols, data types, polling rates, and integration cost. Define accurate OEE methodology: include quality losses, remove buffer padding, and align with what Nissan Japan expects to see.

Outcome: connectivity matrix + OEE definition that reflects operational reality.
Phase 4 — 1 week

Help/Call, downtime & quality KB

Design a Help/Call system that technicians want to use: clear routing, escalation rules, priority logic, and close-out requirements integrated with FAULT. Define requirements for a centralized quality knowledge base so root-cause patterns survive personnel turnover.

Outcome: workflow spec that addresses data adoption at the source + KB requirements.
Phase 5 — 1 week

Traceability & automation

Identify where manual data collection can be reduced through automation — machine signals, barcode/scan events, or structured form capture — and document the traceability requirements and effort-to-value assessment for each opportunity.

Outcome: automation candidates with effort-to-value assessment.
Phase 6 — 1.5 weeks

Functional specification

Compile all workstream outputs into a build-ready functional spec: requirements, architecture, integration points, data flows, and a follow-on implementation estimate scoped to the Fascia & Plastics area.

Outcome: a document that engineering can build from — no discovery re-work needed.
