Overview / Asset Health Index

Asset Health Index — one score, four pillars

The Asset Health Index (AHI) compiles real-time condition, operational reliability, lifecycle age, and financial risk into a single 0–100% score per asset. The database layer — fed by Cimplicity and HighByte — computes it as a weighted moving average tuned for heavy, asset-intensive manufacturing. Below is the blueprint and a real worked example for an ABB paint robot in Cell 056.

The weighting blueprint

Four pillars, one weighted score

Weights are tuned for heavy, asset-intensive manufacturing — condition carries the most signal, financial and age temper it. Pillar 1 is fed by the real OT telemetry templated on the Detail Design tab; pillars 2–4 draw on MES, EAM, and ERP / Maximo.

Worked example

ABB IRB 6600 — Robot BC5604, Paint Cell 056 (base coat)

The highest-burden robot in Cell 056 in June 2026: 11 work orders, 29 labor hours, 7 emergency callouts. Hi-slope faults, paint leaks, max-tracking errors, and HV faults across the month. Here is how its June metrics compile into a single AHI score.

42%ALERT

Asset Health Index = 42%. Reliability is severely degraded — 7 emergency work orders in a single month, repeated applicator failures, vacuum-line contamination, and a color-tree paint leak spanning multiple shifts. The condition pillar is declining; financial risk is high. This asset needs a structured intervention plan, not another reactive repair.

1 · Condition monitoringS₁ = 73%
  • Axis 2 peak torque (June): 28.2 Nm max / −18.2 Nm min — within range but variable across repeated applicator swaps
  • Hi-slope faults: repeated A1Slope:Val hi events — applicator wear pattern consistent with vacuum-line contamination
  • HV faults: multiple high-voltage events in the final week of June
  • Composite condition score = 73%
2 · Operational reliabilityS₂ = 26%
  • Emergency WOs in June: 7 (highest in cell) — MTBF < 4 days
  • Corrective WOs: 3 additional — paint leak (color tree), fill-cart error, applicator rebuild
  • Multi-shift events: color-tree leak persisted across 3 shifts before repair
  • Severely degraded reliability = 26%
3 · Lifecycle ageS₃ = 15%
  • Current runtime: ~43,000 operational hours (estimated)
  • Rated useful life: 50,000 hours (high-velocity paint config)
  • ~85% of intended life consumed → 15% health remaining
4 · Financial riskS₄ = 20%
  • June labor alone: 29 hours × $75/hr = $2,175 emergency labor
  • Life-to-date repair cost: ~$88,000 (overhauls, applicators, O-rings, paint-leak cleanup)
  • Asset replacement value: $110,000
  • Cost ratio = 80% → score = 20%

Reading the score

A single number lets maintenance and operations leadership triage hundreds of assets at a glance, then drill into the pillars. 42% is a clear ALERT band: BC5604 is not just aging — it is actively consuming labor and production time at an unsustainable rate. Seven emergency callouts in one month, a color-tree leak that spanned three shifts, and recurring hi-slope faults point to a systemic failure mode, not isolated incidents.

Standards basis

Grounded in established health-index practice

The AHI approach — compressing condition, reliability, age, and risk into one banded 0–100 score — is consistent with recognized asset-management standards and decades of published prior art. The closest precedents come from regulated, asset-intensive industries that have done exactly this for years.

What we doRecognized basis
Single 0–100 score, banded into health levelsCIGRE/IEEE transformer Health Index (0–100%, 100 = healthy, <25 = poor); Ofgem CNAIM bands HI1–HI5 mapped to Probability of Failure
Combining condition + reliability + age into one compositeCNAIM: Health Score = Initial (age) × Health-Score Factor (condition) × Reliability Factor
Weighted aggregation of heterogeneous factorsWeighted Sum Model — the named method behind most published Health Indices (Naderian & Jahromi, IEEE 2008)
Pillar 1 · Condition monitoringISO 13374 "Health Assessment" functional block; implemented by MIMOSA OSA-CBM
Pillar 2 · Operational reliabilityCNAIM Reliability Factor; ISO 14224 reliability/maintenance data (MTBF/MTTR); SMRP metrics
Pillar 3 · Lifecycle ageCNAIM age-based Initial Health Score (an initial proxy — see caution); ISO 13381 remaining-useful-life prognostics
Pillar 4 · Financial riskNo condition-monitoring HI precedent — maps to asset-management criticality / consequence-of-failure (ISO 55000, ISO 31000). Our extension.
Precedents

Published methodologies this follows

  • Ofgem CNAIM v2.1 — the UK electricity-network regulator's Health Index / Probability-of-Failure methodology. source →
  • CIGRE / IEEE transformer Health Index — recognized industry technical methodology (CIGRE TB 761; Naderian & Jahromi, IEEE 2008). source →
  • ISO 13374 + MIMOSA OSA-CBM — the standard condition-monitoring processing chain with a formal Health Assessment block. source →
  • Process-pump fleet AHI — a non-transformer, rotating-equipment precedent. source →
What is engineering judgment — disclosed

What the standards don't mandate

  • The 35/25/20/20 weights, the four-pillar set, and the weighted-average math are our engineering judgment — no standard mandates them. Composite-indicator guidance (OECD/JRC) treats weights as value judgments that change the verdict, so they're tuned and validated with Nissan.
  • Age ≠ condition. Lifecycle age is only a starting proxy; observed condition must be able to override it (as CNAIM does with caps & collars).
  • Normalization matters. A simple weighted average can mask a single urgent failure mode; we calibrate the scale (and consider a hard condition override) so one critical signal can't be diluted, and tie each band to an action and timescale.
