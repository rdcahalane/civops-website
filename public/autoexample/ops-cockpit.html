The challenge

What is holding the plant back

The plant operates with experienced teams and high-output equipment. But the data infrastructure hasn’t kept pace with the operational complexity. The result is reactive response, OEE numbers that don’t reflect reality, and lost time hunting for answers that already exist — in separate systems, on separate clocks.

Problem 1

OEE numbers don’t reflect reality

Reported OEE excludes quality losses and relies on buffers and manual entry. The methodology needs to be defined accurately before any improvement program can be measured.

Problem 2

Downtime is logged, not understood

Downtime events are recorded but the upstream cause is frequently lost. A stop at one station is logged at the station — not traced to the constraint that caused it.

Problem 3

Quality and throughput are siloed

Quality defects live in inspection systems. Throughput lives in the historian. No single view connects a quality trend to the asset, shift, or part configuration that produced it.

Problem 4

Help/Call is informal

Downtime response depends on who notices and who calls. There is no automatic trigger, no visibility into open calls, and no data on response time by area or shift.

Problem 5

Data is trapped in separate systems

Historian, downtime system, and quality tools each hold part of the picture. Getting a complete view requires manual correlation — too slow for real-time decisions.

Problem 6

No common operating picture

Team members, supervisors, and managers see different versions of performance. The same shift looks different depending on which system you check and when you check it.
