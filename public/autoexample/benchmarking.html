Overview / Benchmarks

Where the OEM stands — the productivity benchmark

Before the architecture, the honest question: how is the OEM performing versus the field? We answer it at two levels. First, the macro: LNS Research's Industrial Productivity Index (IPI) scores 671 public manufacturers — the OEM against its passenger-vehicle peers, the broad auto industry, and the productivity frontier. Then we drill beneath the number into an asset-health & reliability maturity matrix — the operating capabilities that actually produce the gap, benchmarked not just against other automakers but against the industries that mastered reliability first: oil & gas, chemicals, and power. This is an anonymized example; figures are illustrative.

The point of this page: not a report card — a measurable gap. It sizes what the operational-excellence and Unified-Namespace work in the rest of this site exists to close, and gives a number to move.

Reading the curve

Below the pack, and the gap is widening

  • The OEM has run below its passenger-vehicle peer group across the entire 2020–2025 window — never closing to the peer average in any year.
  • The OEM's index peaked at 120 in 2023, then fell ~6 points to 113.9 by 2025 — while the peer group held near 124 and the frontier pulled further ahead (129.5 → 132.3).
  • The result: an 18-point gap to the productivity frontier and a bottom-quartile rank (13th of 16) in the OEM's own peer group.
  • This is the automotive pattern LNS sees broadly — the field's post-2020 productivity recovery separated the leaders from the laggards; the OEM is on the wrong side of that split.

Why this page comes first

A Unified Namespace, a maintenance cockpit, and asset-health scoring aren't the goal — they're the mechanism to move this number. Closing even half the gap to the peer average is measurable margin.

The rest of this site is the plan to do it: the architecture that makes plant data usable, the live proof it runs today, and the ROI that ties it back to this index.

Beneath the number

Why the gap exists — the reliability maturity matrix

The index says how much. This says why. Seven operating capabilities that drive asset uptime and productivity, each scored on the standard maintenance-maturity ladder — Reactive → Preventive → Condition-based → Predictive → Prescriptive. the flagship plant today, the automotive median, the automotive frontier, and — the honest benchmark — the cross-industry reliability frontier: oil & gas, chemicals, and power, the asset-intensive industries that built predictive maintenance and remote operations decades before automotive needed to.

Where the OEM sits

Well-instrumented, under-leveraged

the flagship plant is well-instrumented and already runs predictive monitoring — data and analytics are relative strengths. Yet maintenance stays predominantly reactive (emergency + corrective), with little preventive and effectively no predictive execution. The condition data sits in islands and never closes the loop — so the same faults recur. Monitoring without execution.

The reliability frontier

Prescriptive, from a central desk

Oil & gas and chemicals run remote operations centers that watch fleet health, predict failures, and prescribe the intervention — because unplanned downtime there is measured in millions per hour. That discipline, not a newer robot, is what automotive’s productivity leaders are now importing.

The move this site makes

Build on the instrumentation, close the low points

The plant already has the data. The Unified Namespace turns the flagship plant’s existing instrumentation into one governed, shared record — which directly lifts the two lowest scores, Cross-functional decision flow and Reliability engineering, and makes predictive and prescriptive reachable rather than aspirational.

A different lens than the tech-stack scorecard. A capability benchmark like this scores business outcomes — can the plant find the cause, share the decision, and stop the recurrence? That is deliberately different from an IT/architecture scorecard that rates edge compute, connectivity, digital twins, and cloud as components. Those matter, but a plant can score well on the component checklist and still sit at reliability maturity 2. This matrix measures whether the operating model, not the tech inventory, has moved.
The maintenance signature

How the work actually breaks down

Maturity isn't abstract — it shows up in the maintenance mix. A plant stuck at reactive/preventive spends its hours fighting fires and swapping parts on a calendar; a reliability leader spends them on condition and prediction. No proprietary LNS research-library data is used, and no individual company's maturity score is claimed — the matrix locates the capability gap, it does not assert decimal-precise scores.
