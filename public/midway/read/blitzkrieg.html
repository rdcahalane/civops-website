DISPATCH I · THE LATENCY TEST

Blitzkrieg Was a Decision Architecture That Made Strength Late

France had strength. Germany had the shorter system from signal to legitimate action.

By Michael Carroll

THREE TAKEAWAYS

1. The lesson of 1940 is not that strength stopped mattering. The lesson is that strength becomes late when the operating architecture cannot convert it into coordinated consequence.

2. Speed is not a leadership mood. It is the result of doctrine, permission, communication, concentration, and trust designed before the moment of need.

3. AI will expose the same divide inside enterprises. The better question is not how much intelligence the firm owns. It is whether evidence, reasoning, permission, action, and learning can move together before the value of action decays.

The question was wrong before the tanks moved

France did not enter May 1940 as an empty arsenal.

That is the place to begin, not with German tanks racing across a map. France had soldiers, tanks, artillery, fortifications, rail lines, staff processes, maps, command structures, and officers who had learned painful lessons from the First World War. The country was not unserious. It was not without courage. It was not without material strength.

But the world had changed the question.

When German armored columns pushed through the Ardennes, the French and British were already being drawn toward Belgium. That was not an irrational plan. It came from a theory of the next war shaped by the last one. The better Allied formations moved toward the place the old question had trained them to expect. The decisive pressure came through the place the operating model could not process fast enough.

France did not lose because it had nothing with which to respond. It lost because one system converted movement into consequence faster than the other converted strength into response.

That is the article.

This is not an attempt to be right about 1940 from a comfortable distance. That would be too easy, and mostly useless. The goal is to get the question right before the next enterprise builds more strength into an architecture that cannot move. The useful question is not, who owned the better assets? The useful question is, what made strong assets late?

That question matters now because artificial intelligence is entering companies the way new military technologies entered the twentieth century. It is changing what can be sensed, generated, reasoned over, coordinated, authorized, and learned from. The easy response is adoption. Buy the tools. Train the people. Count the users. Announce the pilots. Show the productivity. Produce more work with less effort.

Some of that matters. None of it proves conversion.

Conversion asks a harder question. What can the operating model now make true that it could not make true before? What decision can move closer to the signal? What human judgment can be protected from administrative residue? What authority can be designed in advance? What work can disappear because the distance that created it has been removed? What loop can close while the moment still has value?

That is the difference between trying to be right and trying to get it right. Being right protects the doctrine. Getting it right tests whether the doctrine still fits the world.

The first false answer

The stronger ledger does not matter if the system cannot convert it into timely consequence.

The first false answer is that Germany simply had better tanks.

That is tidy. It is memorable. It is also not good enough. In several important ways, the Allies had serious armored strength. Some French tanks were better armored and better armed than many German tanks they faced. The French B1 bis, for example, was a formidable machine in the narrow terms leaders often prefer to compare: gun, armor, weight, and survivability.

The problem was not the object by itself. The problem was the architecture around the object.

German armor was concentrated. Radios helped units communicate while moving. Air support helped turn break points into shock. Mission command gave subordinate leaders room to exploit opportunity inside intent instead of waiting for every instruction. The point is not that the German system was perfect. It was not. The point is that it was arranged to turn movement, surprise, and local opportunity into consequence while the opposing system was still assembling a response.

France had learned real lessons from 1914 to 1918. Those lessons deserve respect. Preparation mattered. Firepower mattered. Control mattered. Avoiding another slaughter mattered. No serious person should mock an institution for trying not to repeat trauma it had paid for in blood.

But a lesson can be true and still become late.

That is how serious institutions fail. They do not usually wake up one morning and decide to become obsolete. They survive something difficult. They learn. They standardize. They create controls. They build approval paths, review cadences, reporting systems, committees, escalation routines, and metrics. Each piece has a reason. Each piece looks responsible by itself.

Then the environment changes.

The same controls that once protected the institution begin to protect the institution from seeing. The organization has not become stupid. It has become overfitted to the problem it remembers. It is built to prevent the last failure while the next one moves through a path the old system priced as unlikely.

That is why the stronger ledger can still lose. Capital does not help if it cannot be aimed. Data does not help if it cannot be trusted. People do not help if they are trapped in handoffs. Governance does not help if it only gathers authority after time has become expensive. Software does not help if it accelerates work that the operating model should have removed.

The asset was not the tank. The asset was the action loop.

Speed was architecture, not attitude

Speed is often treated as temperament.

Fast leaders. Fast teams. Fast companies. Fast cultures.

That language is easy, and it hides the mechanism. Germany did not succeed in France because every officer simply had a better sense of urgency. Urgency without architecture becomes noise. Urgency with doctrine, permission, communication, and concentration becomes force.

That is the harder lesson. Speed was not a mood. It was designed into the system.

Panzer formations concentrated armored power instead of scattering it as infantry support. Radios gave moving units a better chance to remain connected. Air support made localized pressure feel larger than the material force on the ground. Mission command gave leaders at the edge enough intent to act when opportunity appeared. The German advance created not only physical movement, but decision crisis.

The French system had strength, but much of that strength had to travel through a slower theory of control. The delay was not always visible as delay. It looked like coordination. It looked like discipline. It looked like prudence. That is why latency is so dangerous. It usually wears respectable clothing.

The same pattern is everywhere in large organizations.

A plant sees a signal, but the decision sits above the people closest to the equipment. A customer problem is visible, but the authority to change the promise lives elsewhere. A supply constraint is known, but the response requires a meeting that will not happen until the next cadence. A safety exposure is recognized, but the organization needs a report before it can allow action. A pricing opportunity appears, but the approval path was designed for annual planning. A field team knows what is happening, but headquarters owns the permission.

People call that governance. Sometimes it is. Often it is unmanaged distance.

Decision latency is the time between first signal and legitimate action. It rarely appears as a line item, but it always shows up in the business. It shows up as excess inventory, expedited freight, overtime, missed demand, quality escapes, rework, customer irritation, safety exposure, management attention, and trust lost slowly enough that no one names the cause.

The ledger records the symptoms separately. The operating architecture created them together.

A serious board should be able to ask a plain question: when a material signal appears at the edge of the enterprise, how many human handoffs sit between recognition and authorized action? Which of those handoffs add judgment? Which only transfer accountability? If the same signal appears on a Friday afternoon, does the organization act from designed doctrine, or does it wait for authority to gather on Monday?

The answer reveals whether the company has control or only reporting.

Business evidence helps only if it changes the question

This is where the military analogy needs discipline. History should not be used as decoration for an opinion. It should disturb the question we were about to ask.

There is useful business evidence here. J. Robert Baum and Stefan Wally studied strategic decision speed and firm performance across hundreds of CEOs. Their work is often reduced to a simple message: faster decisions are better. That is not the useful lesson.

The useful lesson is more architectural.

They distinguished centralized strategic management from decentralized operations management. They also distinguished formalized routines from informalized non-routines. That matters because it moves the conversation past the lazy argument between centralization and decentralization. The question is not whether leaders should push everything to the center or everything to the edge. The question is what kind of decision is this, and where does it belong?

Some decisions should be made once and turned into doctrine. Some should be standardized so the organization does not relitigate the ordinary. Some should move close to the signal because distance destroys value. Some should remain open to judgment because the consequence is moral, irreversible, strategic, or uncertain.

Old firms often reverse that logic. They centralize operational decisions that should live near the work. They relitigate routine work because no evidence threshold has been defined. They push non-routine judgment into forms because the system trusts procedure more than people. Then they call the result discipline.

It is not discipline. It is delay with professional language around it.

A mature enterprise does not worship speed. Some speed is reckless. Some friction is ethical. Some review is necessary. Some decisions should be slow because the consequence deserves it. The target is not speed as a slogan. The target is fit: the right decision at the right distance from the signal, under the right authority, with the right evidence, and the right learning loop.

That is decision architecture.

The drug helped the march. It did not design the army.

The Pervitin point belongs in the story, but it has to be held in its proper place.

German troops used methamphetamine-based stimulants in large quantities. Before the invasion of France, millions of tablets were distributed to Wehrmacht units. The purpose was brutal and simple. Men who could stay awake longer could keep moving longer.

That mattered. The body is part of every operating model, even when the institution pretends it is not. Continuous pressure depends on human endurance. Fatigue changes judgment. Exhaustion changes risk. Pace has a human cost.

But Pervitin did not invent Blitzkrieg.

A pill cannot create mission command. Methamphetamine cannot design a panzer division. It cannot install radios, integrate air support, choose the Ardennes, grant subordinate commanders permission to exploit a break, or teach an army how to turn local opportunity into system-level collapse.

It can amplify an operating model. It cannot substitute for one.

That is the warning for AI.

Many companies are preparing to use AI as digital Pervitin. They will use it to produce more emails, more summaries, more reports, more dashboards, more decks, more reconciliations, more meeting notes, more workflow updates, and more polished explanations of work that should not exist. They will call it productivity. Some of it will help locally. Some people will genuinely get time back. Some work will become easier.

But if the architecture remains unchanged, the system has not been transformed. It has been stimulated.

The real AI question is not whether the model makes work faster. The real question is whether it removes the distance that made the work necessary. If a planner still copies data between systems, reconciles numbers no one trusts, asks three people for approval, and prepares a deck for a meeting where the answer was obvious two days earlier, AI may shorten the typing. It has not created control.

That is not transformation. It is a better stimulant for an exhausted bureaucracy.

Permission is the new armor

Every serious conversation about agentic AI eventually reaches permission.

An agent that can shape an outcome has to operate under legitimate authority. That authority cannot be improvised in the moment of action. It has to be designed before the signal appears. Otherwise the agent becomes either dangerous or useless. Dangerous when it acts without real boundaries. Useless when every meaningful action still waits on the old approval chain.

Good permission is not the enemy of speed. Good permission is how speed becomes legitimate.

Bad permission says stop and ask. Good permission says that when the evidence meets a defined threshold, the action sits inside an approved boundary, the risk class is understood, the record is created, the exception path is known, and reversibility has been considered, the system may move.

That is not weaker governance. It is governance moved upstream.

This is where the One-Degree enterprise separates itself from the digitized old firm. The digitized old firm has modern tools sitting on top of old distance. Signals still travel too far. People still translate between systems. Meetings still gather the authority that the architecture failed to place near the work. Reports still explain what the operating model failed to prevent.

The One-Degree enterprise reduces the separation between evidence, reasoning, permission, action, and learning. It knows where human judgment is essential because the consequence is moral, irreversible, high risk, or strategically contested. It also knows where humans are only being used as middleware between disconnected software, unclear authority, and institutional fear.

Trust has to be treated as architecture, not sentiment.

Trust is not a speech about empowerment. Trust is the designed ability to know what evidence is sufficient, which actor is authorized, which action is allowed, what must be logged, what can be reversed, what must be escalated, and what must never be delegated.

When trust is designed well, speed rises without giving up control. When trust is absent, the enterprise chooses between paralysis and recklessness.

Permission is not the brake. Poorly designed permission is the brake.

The counterargument that deserves respect

There is a counterargument that deserves respect. Military history is not a business case study, and the fall of France should not be flattened into a management metaphor.

That objection is right.

War is human tragedy before it is a lesson. The people who fought in 1940 were not diagram elements. They were sons, fathers, officers, conscripts, civilians, prisoners, commanders, and families caught inside decisions made at national scale. A business article has to be careful with that. The analogy must stay narrow.

The lesson is not that companies are armies. The lesson is not that every business problem should be solved by speed. The lesson is not that friction is always bad. The lesson is that in fast-changing environments, institutions often own serious strength while lacking the architecture to convert that strength into timely, legitimate action.

That mechanism is not confined to war.

It appears in firms, schools, hospitals, governments, and industries whenever the environment changes faster than the doctrine of action. It appears when the organization has visibility without permission. It appears when reporting outruns authority. It appears when technology increases the volume of signals but does not shorten the distance to consequence. It appears when leaders defend the system that made them successful instead of testing whether the system still fits the world.

That is why the better question matters.

The question is not, how do we prove our model is right? The question is, what would we have to see to know our model is becoming late?

A real question should move architecture. It should change a decision right, remove a handoff, clarify an evidence threshold, preauthorize an action, protect human judgment, or retire a ritual. A weak question only produces another meeting.

The next Ardennes is already open

The Ardennes was not merely terrain. It was a test of assumptions.

Allied planners had reasons to discount it as the main route for a major armored attack. The roads were poor. The forest was difficult. Movement through the region could have become a German disaster if blocked and attacked quickly enough. But the Germans did not need the Ardennes to be easy. They needed it to be possible, unexpected, and capable of creating a decision crisis.

Every institution has an Ardennes.

The next exposed route is often the path the incumbent already dismissed.

It is the path the incumbent has judged impractical, too early, too small, too risky, too hard to scale, too strange, too low margin, too immature, or not relevant to the main business. It may be a technology architecture, a pricing model, a labor model, a regulatory opening, a distribution model, a customer behavior, or a competitor with less capital but less latency.

The danger is not only that the incumbent is wrong. The danger is that the incumbent is too slow to recover once the dismissed path becomes real.

The better question is not, what do we expect? Expected events rarely break serious organizations by themselves. The better question is, what would make our strongest assets late?

That question points directly at conversion. Can our capital, talent, systems, data, and relationships be aimed quickly enough when the environment stops matching the plan? Can the edge act inside intent? Can evidence become permission without waiting for ceremony? Can learning return to the architecture before the next signal arrives?

In manufacturing, a smaller competitor may change service levels because planning and production decisions sit closer to demand. In education, a learning system may help a child while the traditional model waits for the assessment cycle. In software, applications may lose value as reasoning and generation move closer to the user's intent. In corporate functions, work once sold as expertise may become embedded in agentic systems that carry the burden without the ceremony.

This does not make people less important. It makes the misuse of people less defensible.

Human judgment becomes more valuable when it is reserved for the moments where judgment matters. A person should not be the API between systems. A manager should not be the cache for missing context. A meeting should not be the database of authority.

The decision architecture test

A serious leadership team does not need another AI slogan. It needs to know where the system is late.

Start with one recurring issue that matters financially, operationally, or morally. Do not start with a technology use case. Start with the distance. How long passes between the first signal and the first authorized intervention? How many people touch the signal before action? What data is reviewed twice? What decision has to be relitigated because authority was never clear? What cost appears downstream because the system waited upstream?

Then ask whether the enterprise has standardized the right work. Which recurring decisions still require meetings because no evidence threshold exists? Which ordinary approvals still consume management time because the policy is vague? Which exceptions should be escalated because the consequence is real? Which non-routine decisions are being forced into routine forms because the enterprise trusts forms more than judgment?

These are not administrative questions. They are performance questions.

Then ask the AI question plainly. If an agent had perfect access to the relevant data but no authority, what would improve and what would remain exactly the same? If the report gets better but action still waits, the constraint is not intelligence. It is permission. If action could occur but no one can explain the evidence threshold, audit trail, exception rule, reversibility, or owner of consequence, the constraint is trust. If no one owns the outcome, the constraint is leadership design, not software.

That is where Blitzkrieg becomes more than a military analogy. Stronger versus weaker is not enough. Better equipped versus poorly equipped is not enough. More data versus less data is not enough. The decisive comparison is conversion rate under pressure. Which system can turn what it knows into legitimate action while action still matters?

The leadership test is whether evidence, permission, action, and learning move together.

France did not become weak in six weeks. Six weeks revealed how much of its strength depended on time it no longer had.

Fast environments do that. They expose the distance between doctrine and reality, authority and action, equipment and consequence, intelligence and permission. They reveal whether leaders built for the world they face or the one they remember.

Do not build more strength into a system that cannot move

That is now the AI test.

Many companies will own capable models. Fewer will redesign the enterprise so evidence, reasoning, permission, action, and learning move together. At first, the old firm may still look stronger. It may have more capital, more data, more people, more systems, more vendors, more committees, and more responsible language around adoption.

The weakness will remain hidden until the environment moves through a path the old model discounted. Then strength will have to prove it can move while the moment still has value.

That was the deeper architecture of Blitzkrieg. Pervitin extended the march, but it did not design the army. Tanks supplied force, but force alone did not create collapse. Radios, doctrine, concentration, permission, and command intent shortened the distance between signal and action. One system converted movement into consequence while the other was still converting understanding into response.

That is the enterprise lesson.

Do not confuse assets with capability. Do not confuse visibility with control. Do not confuse AI adoption with decision velocity. Do not use intelligent software to stimulate an exhausted bureaucracy. And do not ask people to move faster while preserving the architecture that makes timely action illegitimate.

Ask the better question.

What would make our strongest assets late?

Then do something with the answer. Move the decision right. Define the evidence. Design the permission. Protect the human judgment. Remove the handoff. Retire the ritual. Close the learning loop.

Because by the time the ledger proves who was stronger, the decision may already be over.
